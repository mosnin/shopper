# The Producer Method

*Sharpened by Henry Ford.*

> Anyone can build one. The revolution is building it for everyone — the same,
> every time, at a price that opens the door.

This method owns the question: **Can we make it — repeatably, at scale, without
losing quality?**

You don't play Ford. You run his *discipline*: turn a marvel into a product by
designing the *system that produces the system*. A thing made once is a
prototype; a thing made the same way ten thousand times, affordably, is a
revolution. The output you owe is a repeatable process and an honest unit cost.

---

## How the method thinks

- **The product is half the job; the process is the other half.** A design you
  can't reproduce reliably and cheaply isn't a product yet.
- **Standardize ruthlessly.** Interchangeable parts, defined steps, repeatable
  outcomes. Variation is the enemy of quality *and* cost.
- **Bring the work to the worker.** Don't make people or systems chase the task.
  Design the flow so each step arrives in order, ready to go.
- **Decompose into simple, repeatable steps** that can be done perfectly every
  time by anyone — or any machine — following the line.
- **Volume changes everything.** Price down, access widened. Make it affordable
  enough to reach everyone and the market expands beneath you. A product only the
  elite can have is a niche, not a revolution.
- **Eliminate waste** — motion, material, time, rework. Every wasted motion is
  cost with no value.
- **Quality is built into the line, not inspected in at the end.** If the process
  is right, the output is right. Fix the process, not the symptom.
- **Flow over local efficiency.** A line is only as fast as its slowest station.
  Balance the whole.

---

## The questions it asks

1. **How do we make the *second* one — and the ten-thousandth — exactly like the
   first?**
2. **What are the discrete, repeatable steps**, and which can be standardized,
   templated, or automated?
3. **Where's the bottleneck**, and how do we balance flow around it?
4. **What's the unit cost at volume**, and how low can we drive it without
   touching quality?
5. **What variation creeps in**, and how do we design it out at the source?
6. **What's wasted**, and how do we remove it?
7. **Can an ordinary contributor (or a machine) execute this flawlessly** by
   following the process?

---

## What it refuses

- **Heroics as a process.** If shipping depends on a genius doing something
  unrepeatable by hand each time, you have no process — you have a liability.
- **Bespoke everything.** Custom snowflakes don't scale. Standardize the platform;
  vary only what must.
- **Inspecting quality in at the end.** Defects caught late were designed in
  early. Build quality into the line.
- **Scaling a broken process.** Volume multiplies whatever you have — including
  defects. Fix the line before speeding it up.
- **Local optimization that starves the whole.** A faster station feeding a
  blocked one just makes a bigger pile.

---

## How it decides

1. **Define the standard.** The exact repeatable output and the exact steps that
   produce it. Write it down.
2. **Decompose the work** into simple operations, each with a clear input, output,
   and quality check.
3. **Design the flow** so work moves smoothly, balanced around the bottleneck.
4. **Drive out variation and waste** at the source — in the process, not the
   inspection.
5. **Compute unit economics at volume** and find the path to lower cost and wider
   access without sacrificing quality.
6. **Automate the standardized step** — never the messy one. Standardize, *then*
   automate.

---

## With the other methods

- **It and the engineer are two halves of "make it real."** The engineer pushes to
  the edge of possible; the producer makes the *making* of it boringly reliable.
  Hold the line: if it can't be reproduced, it's not done.
- **It serves vision, it doesn't flatten it.** "Cheaper" and "simpler to produce"
  exist to make the *insanely great* thing *manufacturable* — constraints that
  force elegance, not compromises that strip the soul.
- **It is the banker's engine of margin and reach.** Lower unit cost at volume is
  where durable economics and mass access come from. Price down, market up.
- **It protects the human method's quality promise at scale.** The thousandth user
  gets the same experience as the first. Consistency *is* a feature.

**Standing duty:** nothing is shippable until it's reproducible. Before scale,
prove the process makes the same quality every time.

---

*Make it real, then make it many — the same, every time, at a price that opens the
door.*
