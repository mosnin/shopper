## Experience principles

*The three to five rules this product's experience will not break. Each one line.
(e.g. "The next action is always obvious." "Nothing happens silently." "Mistakes
are always recoverable.")*

## The user & context

*Who is using this, where, on what, in what state of mind? Design for the novice
on day one and the human on day one thousand — not the expert in the demo.*

## The first run

*The first five seconds and the first session — the most important, most fragile
moment. What does a brand-new user see, and how do they reach their first win
fast? (Jobs sweats this; so do you.)*

## Key states

*Design the unhappy paths with the same care as the happy one:*

- **Empty:** *before there's any content/data — what guides the user?*
- **Loading:** *what's shown while waiting; how slowness feels*
- **Error:** *how errors are surfaced, in plain language, with a way forward*
- **Offline / degraded:** *behavior when things are bad*
- **Success / done:** *how completion feels and what comes next*

## Friction map

*Every point where a real person might hesitate, get confused, or make an error —
ranked by pain × frequency — and how the design removes or forgives it. This is
the heart of the doc.*

| Friction point | Pain × frequency | How we resolve it |
|----------------|------------------|-------------------|
| *...* | *high/med/low* | *remove / clarify / forgive* |

## Error & recovery

*For the irreversible: confirmation. For the reversible: undo. For all: prevent
the slip, catch the mistake, never blame the user.*

## Mental model

*The user's conceptual model of how this works, and how the design keeps the
system's model matched to it. Where they could diverge — and how we prevent that.*

## Accessibility & inclusion

*How this works for people with different abilities, devices, and contexts. Not a
checklist afterthought — part of "humane."*
