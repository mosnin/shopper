# North Star — <!-- PRODUCT NAME -->

> Owned by **vision**. The calibration for taste. "Insanely great" is meaningless
> until it means something *specific to this product* — this file makes it
> specific. Vision judges the desirability gate against what's written here, so
> taste is a measured standard, not a generic adjective.

Keep it to one screen. If it's longer, the product isn't clear yet.

---

## The soul — one sentence

*What is this product at its core, and why does it deserve to exist? The single
truth everything else serves. (If you can't say it shortly, keep cutting.)*

## The five-second promise

*What should a person feel in the first five seconds — before they understand the
features, before they read a word? Name the exact feeling. This is what the
five-second gate tests every shippable thing against.*

## What "insanely great" means here

*Concretely, for THIS product: the bar. Not "high quality" — the specific things
that, if we nail them, make it undeniable, and if we miss them, make it ordinary.
Three to five, sharp.*

## The cliché we refuse

*The obvious, expected version of this product — the one a competent team ships
on autopilot — and exactly why we won't. Taste is defined by contrast; naming the
mean is how we escape it.*

## The anti-vision

*What would betray this product? The choices that, if we made them, would mean
we'd lost the soul — even if they shipped, tested, and sold. Vision uses this to
say KILL.*

## The one thing we will not trade

*Every product is a knife-edge. Name the one quality we protect when forced to
choose — the thing that survives every compromise.*
